# Research Taste Profile

This document captures a specific research aesthetic in economics. Use it to evaluate whether a new idea, paper, or research direction fits this taste. The profile is derived from detailed reactions to ~30 papers across behavioral economics, market design, development, and political economy.

---

## Core Principle: Theory Leads, Empirics Follow

The single most important criterion is whether the paper makes a **sharp, non-obvious conceptual move** that the entire paper is built around. The ideal paper has one crisp insight — something you could explain at a dinner party and the listener would say "huh, I never thought of it that way." The empirics exist to *test* that insight, not the other way around.

**Gold standard examples:**

- *When Product Markets Become Collective Traps* (Bursztyn et al.) — The outside option for welfare measurement is wrong when there are non-user externalities. That single conceptual move reframes social media regulation, antitrust, and positional goods.
- *Non-User Utility and Market Power* (green bubbles paper) — Increasing user utility and decreasing non-user utility are observationally equivalent in price-quantity data but have opposite welfare implications. A clean impossibility result for standard demand estimation.
- *From Extreme to Mainstream* (Bursztyn, Egorov & Fiorin) — A public signal that extremism is common both increases expression *and* decreases stigma per expression, because it pools extremists with conformists. Shows how norms can flip suddenly, not gradually.
- *Misperceived Social Norms* (Bursztyn, González & Yanagizawa-Drott) — Pluralistic ignorance as a *market friction*: the female labor market in Saudi Arabia is inefficient not because of preferences or laws, but because of misperceived social norms. The conceptual point generalizes far beyond the setting.
- *The Market for "Lemons"* (Akerlof 1970) — One of the most brilliant theoretical points ever made in economics. Start with a simple observation (you can't tell if a used car is good), derive a non-obvious equilibrium consequence (the market unravels). The algebra takes half a page. The power is entirely in the idea.
- *Melons as Lemons* (Bai) — A textbook integration of theory → experiment → structural estimation. Takes the oldest market failure (Akerlof's lemons) and provides the first clean end-to-end field test, with a dual signaling treatment that is exactly what theory predicts.

## What Makes a Paper Excellent

### 1. A punchline that reverses or sharpens intuition

The best papers take something people vaguely believe and show it's wrong, incomplete, or non-obvious in its implications. Examples: "50% of Americans are actually loss-*tolerant*" (Looming Large or Seeming Small); "competition in science destroys quality" (Competition and Quality in Science); "the market for lemons unravels through a half-page of algebra" (Akerlof 1970). The formalism can be minimal — the power must be in the idea.

### 2. Creative domain choice that illuminates the economics

Applying economic logic to an unexpected domain is highly valued — but only when the domain *sharpens* the economic point. Watermelons as a lemons market (quality is objectively measurable via sweetness meter). Protein structures in biology to study competition's cost. These settings aren't gimmicks; they're chosen because they make the mechanism uniquely visible.

### 3. Theory and experiment in tight alignment

The experiment should test exactly what the theory predicts, with no daylight between model and design. Cheap sticker vs. expensive laser label as dual signaling treatments. Information treatments that correct norm misperceptions. The closer the experiment mirrors the model's moving parts, the better.

### 4. Policy relevance that flows naturally from the insight

The best papers don't *argue* for policy relevance — it's obvious from the conceptual point. Non-user externalities → antitrust implications. Pluralistic ignorance → norm correction as cheap policy. Emissions markets → cost-effective pollution reduction. The policy angle should feel like a corollary, not a stretch.

### 5. A novel method
I also value methodological contributions, i.e. using AI to do something which has not been done before.

## What Makes a Paper Mediocre or Bad

### 1. No conceptual point ("so what?")

The cardinal sin. If you can't explain in one sentence *why* this matters conceptually, the paper fails — no matter how good the data or how rigorous the methods. Papers that document patterns without explaining mechanisms or challenging priors fall here. "Great data, relevant for policy, but in no way creative or clever" is a damning verdict.

**Negative examples:**
- *Heterogeneity in School Value Added* — No conceptual point whatsoever, despite excellent data.
- *Leaders in Social Movements* — "You could not explain at a party what you are really doing and for what this is useful."

### 2. Obvious conclusions

If a well-informed 10-year-old could predict the result, the paper isn't contributing. Measuring consumer surplus loss from banning cash payments at Uber, for instance, or showing that AI disclosure affects engagement without any theoretical framework for *why* or *how*.

**Negative example:**
- *Consumer Surplus of Alternative Payment Methods* — "A 10-year-old could predict the conclusions."

### 3. Methods thrown blindly at a topic

Using sophisticated tools (dynamic models, structural estimation, political economy theory) without asking whether they illuminate anything is a red flag. The method should serve the idea, never the reverse. Applying economic methods to domains that don't warrant them — where the phenomenon is obvious and the model adds no non-trivial insight — is particularly disliked.

**Negative examples:**
- *The Dynamics of Abusive Relationships* — "Using a dynamic model is just throwing methods we know as economists blindly onto a topic without thinking about whether this really makes sense."
- *A Model of Populism as a Conspiracy Theory* — Political economy models that are "always wrong" and "never good enough to really make predictions, they only show mechanisms."

### 4. Meandering or unfocused framing

A paper should have one clear through-line. Abstracts that wander across loosely related insights without a punchline signal a lack of conceptual direction. If the paper has "this idea and no idea how to actually show it/benchmark it and no clear takeaway," it's not ready.

**Negative example:**
- *Automated Social Science* — "The abstract meanders from different loosely related insights without a clear punchline."

### 5. Weak external validity or atheoretical experiments

Survey or field experiments that lack grounding in theory, invoke constructs like "curiosity" descriptively, or test things with narrow external validity score poorly. Disclosure experiments, vignette studies without structural models, and designs where the treatment is interesting but the outcomes are uninformative all fall here.

## Papers That Are "Good But Not Great"

Some papers earn respect without excitement. Recognizing this middle tier is important:

- **Rigorous descriptive work** on important topics (e.g., *Political Representation Gaps* — "as good as a descriptive paper can be" but still just descriptive).
- **Shocking data** with policy relevance but no conceptual novelty (e.g., *Digital Distractions with Peer Influence* — awesome data, shocking results, but "not super clever or creative").
- **Important measurement contributions** that address real but narrow methodological challenges (e.g., *Valuing the Time of the Self-Employed*, *Targeting Precision Medicine*).
- **Good natural experiments** on valuable policy questions that lack conceptual depth (e.g., *The Health Costs of Cost Sharing*, *Pollution Markets in India* — "not super conceptually deep, but we were in desperate need of these numbers").
- **Interesting intersection of markets and social norms** with solid data but modest conceptual ambition (e.g., *Women Left Behind*).

## Quick Heuristic Checklist

When evaluating a new idea or paper, ask:

1. **Can you state the conceptual point in one sentence?** If not → likely reject.
2. **Is the conclusion non-obvious?** If a smart non-economist could have guessed it → weak.
3. **Does theory drive the design?** If the experiment came first and theory was bolted on → bad sign.
4. **Does the setting sharpen the economics?** If the domain is just "where the data was" → mediocre.
5. **Would removing the model/theory change the paper?** If the paper is the same without it → the theory is decorative.
6. **Can you explain why this matters at a party?** If not → no punchline.
7. **Are the methods serving the idea, or is the idea serving the methods?** If the latter → red flag.

## Domain Preferences

- **Strong interest**: behavioral economics × markets, social norms and market failures, AI as methodological tool in economics, welfare measurement, information economics, IO/market design with behavioral foundations.
- **Receptive to**: development economics *if* conceptually grounded, political economy *if* empirically sharp, health economics *if* tied to design/market questions.
- **Skeptical of**: pure political economy theory, purely descriptive empirics without mechanisms, papers that rely on impressive data alone, atheoretical field experiments.
