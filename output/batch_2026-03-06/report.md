# Research Idea Seeds — Top 10

### #1: Preference Laundering Through Algorithms: How People Use Recommendation Systems to Consume Content They Cannot Admit Wanting
**Question:** Do consumers strategically manipulate their algorithmic profiles to receive recommendations for content they desire but find socially or personally unacceptable to actively seek, and does this "preference laundering" increase welfare?
**Key insight:** This connects to the idea of preferences people cannot admit to themselves (like closeted sexuality). Algorithmic recommendations create a psychologically distinct consumption pathway—"the algorithm showed me this" provides plausible deniability both socially and to oneself. This means recommendation algorithms serve a function that existing models completely miss: they are commitment devices for consuming taboo-adjacent goods. This reframes the "filter bubble" debate entirely.
**Identification:** Design an experiment on a content platform where users are randomized into (a) active search only, (b) algorithmic feed only, or (c) both, measuring consumption of pre-categorized sensitive content (political out-group media, mental health content, gender-atypical content) and post-consumption self-reports of satisfaction and attribution ("I chose this" vs. "it was recommended").

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has exactly the structure this researcher prizes most: a sharp conceptual move ("algorithms as preference laundering devices") that reframes an entire literature (filter bubbles), directly connects to their stated fascination with preferences people cannot admit to themselves (point 5 in their vision), and leads naturally to a clean experimental design. It mirrors the architecture of papers they rated highest—like "Collective Traps" and "Erosion of Social Norms"—where a single theoretical insight reshapes how we think about welfare measurement, and empirics follow from the theory. The connection to market design (how should recommendation algorithms be designed if they serve this hidden function?) gives it the system-level implication they acknowledge lacking.
**Biggest risk:** The biggest risk is measurement: it may be nearly impossible to cleanly distinguish genuine "preference laundering" from simple low-effort browsing or curiosity, and self-reports about taboo consumption are precisely the domain where survey responses are least reliable—the very phenomenon the paper studies (inability to admit preferences) undermines the ability to measure it.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Determinants of social desirability bias in sensitive surveys: a literature review" (closest thematically, though still distant). The core mechanism proposed here — that users *strategically manipulate* algorithmic profiles to launder socially or self-unacceptable preferences, using recommendation systems as plausible deniability infrastructure — is not addressed in any existing paper in this set or, to my knowledge, in the broader recommendation systems/behavioral economics literature. Existing work on filter bubbles, social desirability bias, and taboo consumption treats these as separate phenomena; this idea uniquely synthesizes them into a welfare-relevant framework where algorithms serve as commitment/deniability devices.

### #2: When the Market Discovers Your Secret: The Welfare Effects of Algorithmic Outing in Insurance Markets
**Question:** Does the increasing ability of insurers to predict previously unobservable risk types (via wearables, genetic data, digital footprints) improve or destroy welfare, and does the answer depend on whether the insured *themselves* knew their type?
**Key insight:** Classical adverse selection (Akerlof) assumes agents know their own type—but what if people are in denial or genuinely ignorant about their health risks, and algorithms reveal this? The welfare calculus flips: information revelation may cause psychological harm (forced self-knowledge) even as it solves adverse selection. For people who strategically avoid health information (as documented in the "ostrich effect" literature), algorithmic typing constitutes a form of involuntary outing analogous to revealing sexual orientation. Markets can "work" in the efficiency sense while making participants worse off.
**Identification:** Use a large insurer's introduction of wearable-based premium discounts as a natural experiment. Compare take-up, subsequent health-seeking behavior, and mental health claims between enrollees who receive "surprising" risk feedback (predicted risk diverges from self-assessed risk, measured pre-enrollment) vs. those whose predictions confirm self-assessments.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has exactly the structure this researcher prizes most: a sharp conceptual move (the welfare calculus of information revelation flips when agents don't know their own type) that reframes a canonical model (Akerlof/Rothschild-Stiglitz) and connects to their deep interest in preferences people hide from themselves (point 5 in their vision—"opinions against their will," homosexuality analogy). The "algorithmic outing" framing is vivid and policy-relevant, akin to the "collective traps" and "green bubbles" papers they rated as gold standard—theory leads, empirics follow, with a single devastating conceptual point. It also touches on market design (point 1), market failure (point 2), and what we should optimize for (point 4: efficiency vs. well-being), which are all central to their research vision.
**Biggest risk:** The empirical strategy hinges on observing "surprising" risk feedback at a granular level within a real insurer's data—getting access to both pre-enrollment self-assessed risk *and* algorithmic risk scores *and* subsequent mental health claims may be practically infeasible, and without this the paper becomes a theory piece that this researcher (who dislikes pure theory models like the populism paper) would find less compelling.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Genetic testing, adverse selection, and the demand for life insurance" (closest thematically among the listed papers). The existing literature on genetic testing and insurance focuses on cases where individuals *choose* to learn their type and then act on it strategically — the adverse selection framing assumes agent awareness. This proposal's core novelty is the "involuntary outing" mechanism: algorithmic revelation to insurers of risk types that the insured themselves did not know or actively avoided knowing, combining the ostrich effect literature with insurance economics to show welfare can decrease even when adverse selection is technically resolved. The natural experiment design comparing "surprised" vs. "confirmed" enrollees based on pre-enrollment self-assessed vs. algorithmically-predicted risk divergence is also methodologically distinct from anything in the existing literature.

### #3: Pluralistic Ignorance in Carbon Markets: Do Firms Pollute More Because They Think Competitors Won't Comply?
**Question:** Do firms in emissions trading schemes over-pollute not because abatement is too costly but because they misperceive the compliance intentions of other firms, paralleling the Saudi Arabia WWOH mechanism in an industrial context?
**Key insight:** The Bursztyn et al. (2020) mechanism of misperceived social norms has only been applied to individual social behavior—but it should operate powerfully among firms in nascent pollution markets (like India's Gujarat ETS), where managers privately support compliance but assume competitors will cheat, rationalizing their own non-compliance. This would imply that information interventions about peer compliance could substitute for costly monitoring.
**Identification:** Partner with GPCB or a similar pollution control board to run an RCT providing plant managers with accurate information about aggregate compliance rates of other firms; measure permit purchases, emissions, and elicited beliefs about peer behavior before and after treatment.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea hits several of the researcher's strongest enthusiasms simultaneously: it takes a proven conceptual mechanism (pluralistic ignorance as market friction, which they called "super great" in the Saudi Arabia paper) and transplants it into a new, policy-critical domain (pollution markets, which they praised in the Gujarat ETS paper as "extremely relevant"). The structure mirrors what they described as the "gold standard"—a clean conceptual move leading empirics—and connects market failure (externalities) with social norm misperception, which is precisely at the intersection of their stated research interests #2 and #5. The researcher would also appreciate that the RCT design is concrete and actionable given their experimental skills.
**Biggest risk:** The analogy between individual social behavior (Saudi women's labor supply) and firm-level compliance decisions may be weaker than it appears—firm managers' pollution decisions are driven by cost-benefit calculations, regulatory enforcement, and board-level strategy rather than social norm conformity, so the pluralistic ignorance mechanism may simply not bite in this context, making the null result the most likely outcome.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "AN EXPERIMENTAL ANALYSIS OF COMPLIANCE BEHAVIOR IN EMISSIONS TRADING PROGRAMS: SOME PRELIMINARY RESULTS" (author not listed in abstract). The existing experimental compliance literature focuses on cost-benefit determinants of compliance decisions, not on misperceived peer behavior as a mechanism driving non-compliance. This proposal uniquely combines the Bursztyn et al. pluralistic ignorance mechanism with emissions trading compliance, proposing that correcting belief misperceptions about peer compliance (rather than changing penalties or costs) can substitute for costly monitoring—a theoretically and empirically distinct contribution.

### #4: The Auction Nobody Wanted: Why Emergency Room Triage Is a Misdesigned Priority Queue
**Question:** Would replacing subjective ER triage (a priority queue with noisy signals) with a mechanism-design-inspired system where patients report their own pain/urgency under incentive-compatible elicitation improve welfare and reduce racial disparities in wait times?
**Key insight:** ER triage is effectively a poorly designed allocation mechanism where the "auctioneer" (triage nurse) estimates values with massive noise and known racial bias. Mechanism design tells us we should elicit private information from the agents themselves—but medicine has never framed this as a market design problem. The non-obvious claim is that a well-designed self-report mechanism with spot-checks could dominate expert assessment, especially for conditions where pain is the primary input.
**Identification:** Run a field experiment in a partner hospital's ER comparing standard triage with an incentive-compatible self-report tool (e.g., patients choose between "fast track with mandatory follow-up cost" vs. "standard wait"), measuring wait times, re-admission rates, and demographic disparities.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has the "theory leads, empirics follow" structure this researcher loves (cf. their reaction to the Collective Traps and Melons as Lemons papers): a crisp conceptual reframing (ER triage as a misdesigned mechanism) that generates a non-obvious empirical prediction (incentive-compatible self-report can dominate expert assessment). It hits squarely on their core interests—market design in non-standard settings, information asymmetry, and the interaction of social norms/bias with allocation mechanisms. The racial disparity angle adds real-world stakes similar to what they admired in the Saudi Arabia norms paper, and the field experiment design leverages their experimental skills and company connections (Bloit Inc. for health-adjacent partnerships).
**Biggest risk:** The biggest risk is feasibility and institutional resistance—getting IRB approval and hospital buy-in for a field experiment that alters emergency medical triage is extraordinarily difficult, and the ethical/legal constraints may force such severe design compromises that the clean theoretical insight gets buried in implementation noise, turning this into exactly the kind of "no clear punchline" paper the researcher dislikes.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Fairness of artificial intelligence in healthcare: review and recommendations" (author details not provided in abstract). While existing literature addresses racial bias and AI fairness in healthcare, no identified paper frames ER triage as a mechanism design/market design problem and proposes incentive-compatible self-report elicitation as a structural solution. The core theoretical contribution—treating triage as a misdesigned priority queue where private information should be elicited from patients rather than estimated by noisy expert assessors—appears genuinely absent from the literature.

### #5: Product Market Traps in Education: Do Students Stay in Prestigious Programs They'd Prefer Not to Exist?
**Question:** Analogous to the Allcott et al. TikTok/Instagram collective trap, do students enrolled in elite-but-miserable degree programs (law, MBA, medicine) exhibit negative welfare when accounting for the externality that others' enrollment forces them to participate?
**Key insight:** The "collective trap" framework from social media applies naturally to positional goods in education—where the value of not having a credential is endogenous to how many peers hold one. The surprising prediction is that students in the most prestigious programs may have the most negative adjusted welfare, because the positional externality is strongest precisely where the credential is most valuable. This reframes the "rat race" as a measurable market failure.
**Identification:** Run incentivized WTA/WTP experiments with students at a partner university (e.g., through PumpkinCareers' student consulting network) measuring: (a) private valuation of their own enrollment, (b) WTP for a world where the program doesn't exist for anyone, yielding Allcott-style adjusted surplus measures across programs.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea directly mirrors the "Collective Traps" paper (which they called "the gold standard of what I aspire to") and applies the same conceptual move—correcting the outside option for non-user externalities—to a new, relatable domain. The researcher values "theory leads, empirics follow" and clean conceptual points with sharp experimental designs, and this delivers exactly that structure. The PumpkinCareers connection makes it feasible and fast, which aligns with their strength in speed and experimental skills. It loses a point because it's quite obviously derivative of Allcott et al.—the researcher might worry it's "applying someone else's idea" rather than making a genuinely novel conceptual contribution, and they tend to penalize papers that lack a surprising punchline beyond the original framework.
**Biggest risk:** The main risk is that the analogy is too direct—a referee (or the researcher themselves) might say "this is just Allcott et al. in a different setting" without a new theoretical insight, and the researcher explicitly dislikes papers without a clear conceptual contribution beyond replication. The education setting also introduces confounds (human capital vs. signaling) that make the welfare interpretation murkier than in the social media case.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Relative Income, Happiness and Utility: An Explanation for the Easterlin Paradox and Other Puzzles" (Clark, Frijters, Shields). While the relative income/positional goods literature documents welfare losses from status competition, this proposal uniquely operationalizes Allcott et al.'s specific "collective trap" welfare measurement framework (WTA/WTP for a world where the good doesn't exist for anyone) to quantify adjusted surplus in elite education markets—yielding a concrete, incentivized experimental test of whether prestigious credentials constitute measurable market failures analogous to social media addiction. No existing paper applies this specific welfare accounting methodology to credentialing externalities.

### #6: Confessing to the Algorithm: Do People Reveal Stigmatized Preferences More Honestly to AI Than to Human Surveyors?
**Question:** Does replacing a human interviewer with an AI chatbot in sensitive survey contexts (sexual orientation, drug use, political extremism) reduce social desirability bias, and does this effect vary with respondents' beliefs about AI's capacity for judgment?
**Key insight:** Survey methodology has long struggled with social desirability bias; list experiments and RRT are partial fixes. The non-obvious insight is that AI interviewers might function as a "judgment-free" elicitation device—but only if respondents don't anthropomorphize the AI. This creates a testable interaction: the bias-reduction effect of AI should be largest for people who score lowest on AI anthropomorphism, completely inverting the naive prediction that people who "trust" AI more would reveal more.
**Identification:** Run a between-subjects field experiment via Bloit Inc.'s AI-psychotherapy platform (or similar) randomizing whether sensitive intake questions are asked by a human therapist, an AI with humanlike avatar, or a clearly-labeled minimal AI interface; measure disclosure rates against validated benchmarks (e.g., known population base rates for substance use from biomarker studies).

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea hits squarely at several of the researcher's core interests: revealing stigmatized/hidden preferences (point 5 in their vision), methodological advances in experimental/survey research (point 3), and leveraging their connection to Bloit Inc. (AI-psychotherapy). The "inverted prediction" about anthropomorphism is exactly the kind of non-obvious conceptual move they celebrated in the Collective Traps and Saudi Arabia papers—theory leads, empirics follow. The researcher would also appreciate that this is technically executable with their skills and connections, avoiding their weakness of limited research networks.
**Biggest risk:** The conceptual point, while clever, may not be deep enough to sustain a top paper—the anthropomorphism interaction is testable but could easily yield null or noisy results, and without a strong structural/equilibrium implication (e.g., what market or institution does this change?), it risks being a "nice methodological finding" rather than the kind of paper with a devastating punchline they admire most. It could end up looking like the disclosure field experiment they criticized (paper 28) if the theoretical framing isn't sufficiently sharp.
**Novelty:** partially_novel — nearest paper: "How Personification and Interactivity Influence Stress-Related Disclosures to Conversational Agents" (the exploratory study with N=441). The core interaction hypothesis — that anthropomorphism of AI *moderates* disclosure rates such that low-anthropomorphizers reveal *more* to minimal AI interfaces, inverting the naive prediction — is not present in existing work, which treats personification as a straightforward positive or negative moderator rather than testing it against individual-difference beliefs about AI judgment. Additionally, the proposed validation against external biomarker benchmarks (rather than self-report comparisons alone) and the explicit focus on highly stigmatized domains (sexual orientation, drug use, political extremism) goes beyond what existing chatbot-disclosure studies have examined.

### #7: Outside Options You Can't Take: How Phantom Alternatives Inflate Bargaining Power in Gig Platforms
**Question:** Do gig economy platforms (Uber, DoorDash) strategically display earnings information from alternative gigs that workers cannot practically access—creating "phantom outside options" that paradoxically increase workers' subjective continuation value and reduce quit rates?
**Key insight:** Standard bargaining theory (as in the Hwang paper) treats outside options as real. But platforms may exploit a behavioral wedge: showing workers that "drivers in your area earn $X on Platform Y" makes workers feel they have options, increasing their sense of control and reducing actual search—the opposite of what a rational model predicts. This is a novel form of "choice architecture as labor discipline" that inverts the classical role of outside options.
**Identification:** Collect screenshots of in-app notifications from gig workers (via a browser extension/survey panel of ~500 gig workers), link to GPS-based labor supply data and actual platform-switching behavior; exploit variation across cities in the availability of competing platforms to test whether "phantom" information (competitor info when no real competitor exists locally) reduces churn.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has exactly the kind of structure this researcher admires most: a sharp conceptual inversion ("phantom outside options" flip the classical bargaining prediction) that leads empirics, similar to the Collective Traps paper they called "the gold standard." It connects market design, behavioral wedges, and platform power in a way that's both theoretically crisp and practically relevant—touching their interests in how markets fail (#2), how people actually make decisions (#5), and what we should optimize for (#4). The researcher would also appreciate that this is explainable at a party: "Uber shows you fake options to keep you from quitting."
**Biggest risk:** The core empirical identification is fragile—it's very hard to prove that platforms *strategically* display competitor earnings information as a retention tool rather than as a benign feature, and the "phantom" classification (real vs. inaccessible alternatives) requires strong assumptions about what workers can practically access. The researcher tends to penalize papers where the conceptual claim outruns the empirical design (see their critique of the Automated Social Science paper for "no clear takeaway"), and this could easily become a suggestive correlation study without a clean experiment.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Algorithmic management and app‐work in the gig economy: A research agenda for employment relations and HRM" (closest thematically, though still quite distant). The core mechanism proposed here—that platforms strategically display *unattainable* competitor information to create "phantom outside options" that paradoxically reduce worker search/quit behavior through an illusion of control—is genuinely novel and not addressed in the existing literature. It inverts classical outside-option theory in bargaining and combines behavioral economics with platform labor strategy in a way that has no direct precedent in the retrieved papers or, to my knowledge, the broader gig economy literature.

### #8: Dead Drugs Walking: How Patients' Causal Models of Their Own Illness Predict Medication Non-Adherence Better Than Price
**Question:** Do patients' folk causal models of disease mechanisms (e.g., "my body builds tolerance" or "I only need medication when symptomatic") explain more variance in drug non-adherence than out-of-pocket costs, even among those facing the Medicare Part D coverage gap?
**Key insight:** The Einav et al. "Health Costs of Cost Sharing" paper shows cost-sharing kills—but the finding that only one-third of patients believe missing drugs for a month matters suggests the binding constraint may not be price but causal beliefs. If patients' mental models of pharmacology are the first-order problem, then the optimal policy is information design, not subsidy design. This reframes a classic market failure (moral hazard/cost-sharing) as fundamentally an epistemic failure.
**Identification:** Augment CMS Medicare Part D claims data with a survey measuring patients' causal models of their specific conditions (elicited via causal diagram tasks), then estimate heterogeneous treatment effects of the coverage gap on adherence/mortality as a function of causal model accuracy.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea follows the researcher's favorite template—"theory leads, empirics follow"—by taking a sharp conceptual reframing (the binding constraint on medication adherence is epistemic, not financial) and building a clean empirical design around it. It directly extends a paper they already admire (Einav et al.), connects to their deep interest in how people's causal models drive economic behavior (theme 5), and leverages their strong survey/experimental skills (eliciting causal diagrams). The idea also has the "explain at a party" quality they value: "patients don't stop taking drugs because they're expensive—they stop because they think the drugs don't work the way they actually do."
**Biggest risk:** The empirical identification is fragile—causal model accuracy is endogenous to education, health literacy, severity, and physician quality, so even with the quasi-random coverage gap variation, it may be very hard to credibly separate the causal belief channel from correlated unobservables, leaving the paper as a suggestive correlation rather than the clean "theory + sharp experiment" the researcher aspires to.
**Novelty:** partially_novel — nearest paper: "Medication adherence as a learning process: insights from cognitive psychology" (closest match from the list, though the broader literature on illness representations/IPQ by Leventhal et al. is the true nearest neighbor). The genuinely novel contribution is the specific methodological combination: using formal causal diagram elicitation tasks to measure folk pharmacological models, then estimating heterogeneous treatment effects of the Medicare Part D coverage gap (a natural experiment with mortality outcomes) as a function of causal model accuracy. The existing literature (Health Belief Model, Leventhal's illness representation work, necessity-concerns framework) has extensively documented that beliefs predict adherence, but none exploit a clean economic quasi-experiment to pit causal beliefs against price as competing explanations for non-adherence with hard outcomes (mortality). The reframing from "market failure" to "epistemic failure" as a policy-relevant decomposition is also underexplored, though the conceptual ground (beliefs matter) is well-trodden.

### #9: The Unraveling of Anonymous Peer Review: How AI Detection Accusations Create a New Lemons Problem in Academic Publishing
**Question:** Does the suspicion that submitted papers may be AI-generated cause reviewers to systematically penalize well-written papers by unknown authors, creating an adverse selection dynamic that favors established researchers?
**Key insight:** Akerlof's lemons logic applies in reverse: when quality signals (clear prose, comprehensive literature reviews) become associated with AI use, the market for ideas penalizes exactly the papers that look "too good" from newcomers. This generates a perverse equilibrium where junior researchers strategically write worse to signal authenticity, while established researchers face no such penalty due to reputation capital. This is a novel information asymmetry created by technology.
**Identification:** Submit pairs of identical papers (varying only author reputation signals: unknown vs. established-sounding affiliations) to a simulated peer review exercise with ~500 active reviewers recruited via Prolific, randomizing whether papers include a disclaimer about AI non-use, and measuring review scores and explicit AI suspicion flags.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has exactly the structure this researcher prizes most: a clean conceptual move (Akerlof's lemons logic inverted by AI suspicion) that leads empirics, applied to a real and timely market failure. The researcher loved the "Collective Traps" and "Melons as Lemons" papers precisely because they took a crisp theoretical insight and built a tight experiment around it, and this idea follows that template. The connection to tacit knowledge (what signals "authentic" writing?) and the researcher's interest in how markets fail due to information asymmetry and social norms makes this particularly well-aligned.
**Biggest risk:** The core empirical challenge is that a simulated peer review exercise on Prolific with hypothetical papers may lack external validity—the researcher has explicitly flagged dislike for "disclosure things" and experimental designs without strong external validity (see reaction to paper [28]). Real reviewers evaluating real submissions behave very differently from recruited Prolific workers evaluating hypothetical papers, and the effect size of "AI suspicion penalizing good writing" may be too small or confounded to cleanly identify in a stylized setting, undermining the devastating simplicity the conceptual point deserves.
**Novelty:** novel — nearest paper: "Artificial Intelligence in Peer Review: Enhancing Efficiency While Preserving Integrity" (closest thematically, though abstract is vague). The existing literature examines AI's role in conducting or assisting peer review, but none applies Akerlof's lemons framework to study how AI-suspicion creates adverse selection dynamics that differentially penalize junior versus established researchers based on reputational capital. The specific experimental design testing strategic writing quality penalties as authenticity signals is entirely absent from the literature.

### #10: The Price of Performing Poverty: How Means-Testing Bureaucracy Functions as a Signaling Game That Selects Against the Working Poor
**Question:** Does the administrative burden of means-tested programs (documentation, wait times, repeated verification) function as a separating equilibrium that screens out precisely those low-income individuals with the highest opportunity cost of time—namely, the self-employed and gig workers?
**Key insight:** Existing work frames administrative burden as a cost; the reframing here is that it's an inadvertent auction mechanism that allocates benefits based on willingness-to-wait rather than need. Combined with the Hamory et al. insight that the self-employed's time is poorly valued, this means current welfare systems are systematically biased against the informally employed. This is a market design failure hiding inside bureaucratic design.
**Identification:** Use India's Bhamashah program (as in Dupas et al.) or a comparable means-tested program with administrative data on application attempts, completion rates, and time-to-enrollment, linked to occupational data. Exploit variation in local office processing times (e.g., staff shortages) as an instrument for administrative burden.

---
**Why this fits you:** This idea has the kind of sharp conceptual reframing that this researcher consistently prizes—"theory leads, empirics follow"—recasting administrative burden as an inadvertent auction/signaling mechanism rather than just a cost. It connects beautifully to several of their stated interests: market design failures (stream 1/2), the Hamory et al. time-valuation paper they liked, and the Dupas et al. gender disparities paper they found interesting. The "you could explain it at a party" test passes cleanly: welfare systems accidentally auction off benefits to whoever can afford to wait longest, which selects against the working poor. However, it falls slightly short of a 5 because the empirical strategy (IV on processing times) feels somewhat standard and the researcher might worry this is more of a "nice reframing of known facts" than a genuinely surprising equilibrium result like the Collective Traps or Norms Erosion papers.
**Biggest risk:** The core conceptual move—that administrative burden is a screening mechanism—has already been articulated in the "ordeal mechanisms" literature (e.g., Nichols & Zeckhauser 1982) and recent work by Herd & Moynihan on administrative burden, so the reframing may not be as novel as it appears, which would undercut the paper's main selling point for this researcher who demands genuine conceptual originality.
**Novelty:** partially_novel — nearest paper: "Targeting of Transfers in Developing Countries" by Coady, Grosh, and Hoddinott. While the administrative burden literature (Herd, Moynihan, and others not listed here) documents compliance costs as barriers, this paper's specific reframing as an *inadvertent auction/signaling mechanism that systematically screens out informally employed and gig workers* with high opportunity cost of time—and the proposed instrumental variables strategy using local office processing time variation—is a novel theoretical and empirical contribution not captured by the targeting literature. The signaling game/separating equilibrium framing applied specifically to informal/gig workers is the genuinely new angle, though the broader "administrative burden as barrier" literature (Bhargava & Manoli, Deshpande & Li) comes closer than any paper listed here.
