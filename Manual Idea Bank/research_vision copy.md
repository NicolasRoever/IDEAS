# Research Statement

I am a microeconomist. This means my focus is on understanding particular aspects of economic behavior and decision-making in markets. I have a strong foundation in experimental methods and computational methods—and I am happy to complement them with applied microeconometrics.

## My overall research vision

Economics is the science of the most important system we have to organize a human society: the market. 
There are a few economic streams I find inspiring: 
1. Understanding which market design is appropriate in given situations. Maybe an auction is the best choice, if yes, why? if no, why not?
2. Understanding why markets fail and how to fix them. Classical problems are information asymmetry, externalities, and public goods. But there are many more, such as pluralistic ignorance, which occurs because markets are embedded into  our social lives and norms. 
3. Contributing to methodological advances in (experimental economics), such as how to address bias in survey and experimental research, or how AI can be of help to gathering data better
4. Thinking about what we actually want to optimize for as a society. Social cohesion, individual freedom, equality, well-being, economic growth, or something else? And how to design markets that optimize for these goals. Often they are in conflict to each other and economists are somehow confused how to reconcile them.
5. Really understand how people make their decisions in economic environments---they are influenced by prices, narratives, social norms and their own causal models. Particularly interesting are cases when people make decisions against their will/express opinions, for instance because of social pressure or because they even do not want to admit to themselves that they have a certain preference like homosexuality. 
6. Connecting to that, I am intrigued by "tacit" knowledge, which people act on, for instance their research taste. 

## My Finalized Projects

(1) **The Social Desirability Atlas**, a methodological review on how to address one of the common forms of bias in survey and experimental research, called social desirability bias.

(2) **Evaluating Behavioral Interventions at Scale**. This study takes a fundamental question: How can people be persuaded? And uses AI to deliver three different interviews. We document that the most effective script is one that uses self-generated arguments for change as well as clear guidance and suggestions to realize the goal.

(3) **Bargaining**: An experimental study evaluating the different theories of bargaining.

(4) **AI Against Anxiety: Efficacy, Mechanisms, and Demand for Voice-Based AI Therapy**. Definitely my most exciting project, showing the efficacy of CBT delivered by a voice-based AI relative to a waitlist control. We also dive into the relative comparison between human-delivered and AI-delivered services, showing that AI is low-barrier, and does not engender shame. Meaning that AI is a very nice entry point. This actually is coherent with my own observations. It is easy to talk about very personal things (sexual desires) with an AI—and hard to do it with a human.

(5) **Text analysis of AI-CBT: Which components work, which do not**. 

## Upcoming Work

(1) **Teletherapy vs. AI-therapy**: This project is a follow-up to the AI-CBT project, looking at AI-delivered CBT in one arm vs. CBT delivered by a human therapist via videocall in the other arm.
